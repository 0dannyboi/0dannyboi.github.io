Dynamical Systems

Introduction

Dynamical systems is the branch of mathematics that studies the behavior of a system under the repeated application of a set of rules. Some example use cases include: modeling the population of an ecosystem containing predators and prey, describing the set of configurations a double pendulum can occupy, and modeling the convection of a fluid being heated.

In the most rudimentary cases, we can place a dynamical system into one of two groups based on how the system evolves over time. If the changes occur in countable steps such that the configuration at a step or iteration, \(x_i\), determines the configuration of the following iteration, \(x_{i+1}\), the system is discrete. If we cannot divide the behavior of the system into discrete steps, the dynamics are continuous. In such a case, the evolution of the system is typically modeled by differential equations.

Discrete Dynamical Systems

Some of the simplest dynamical systems take the form of discrete recurrence relations, where the value at some integer step, \(x_n\), depends on the value at the previous integer step, \(x_{n - 1}\). A common definition that uses this notation is the factorial function defined over the non-negative integers:

\[(n+1)! = (n+1) \cdot n! \]

Where \(0!\) is defined as \(1\). Starting from this value, it is easy to define the factorial function for the consecutive integers: \[ 1! = 0! * 1 = 1 * 1 \xrightarrow{} 1 \text{, } \] \[ 2! = 1! * 2 = 1 * 2 \xrightarrow{}2 \text{, } \] \[ 3! = 2! * 3 = 2 * 3 \xrightarrow{} 6 \text{ ...} \]

The prototypical example of a discrete dynamical system is the logistic map, which describes the dynamics of a population in terms of a reproduction rate, \(r\). At any particular iteration, \(n\), we can define the ratio of the population to the systemâ€™s carrying capacity as \(x_n\), a value between 0 and 1.

\[ x_{n+1} = r * x_n (1 - x_n)\]

Intuitively, the reproduction rate tends to increase the population. However, the model accounts for resource scarcity through the factor \((1 - x_n)\), which decreases linearly with the population. As a result, the population tends to increase linearly as \(x_{n+1} \approx r * x_n \) for small populations. As the population approaches the carrying capacity ( \( x_n \sim 1 \) ), the resource scarcity factor approaches 0, driving the population towards 0. Further pages investigate the logistic map's dynamics, showing a range of rich features including chaos.

Continuous Dynamical Systems

A simple continuous dynamical system can be realized by considering a pendulum consisting of a mass, \( m \), suspended on a massless rod of length \(L \). The equations of motion can be obtained by computing the Lagrangian, \(\mathcal{L} = T - U\) and evaluating the Euler-Lagrange equation: \[ \frac{d }{d t} \frac{\partial \mathcal{L} }{\partial \dot{\theta}} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta} \] Where the kinetic energy of the system is: \[ T = \frac{m L^2}{2} \dot{\theta}^2 \] and the potential energy of the system is: \[ U = m g L (1 - \cos(\theta)) \] The equation of motion of the system becomes: \[ \ddot{\theta} = - \frac{g}{L} \sin(\theta) \] We can rewrite this second order differential equation as a coupled set of first order ODEs: \[\frac{d \theta}{d t} = \dot{\theta} \] \[\frac{d \dot{\theta}}{d t} = - \frac{g}{L} \sin(\theta) \] Further pages show how this representation allows us to visually represent the dynamics of this system.
